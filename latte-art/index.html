The perfect texture

You are after wet paint: glossy, silky, with no bubbles you can see. Two moves get you there.

Stretch

In the first two to three seconds, keep the tip just under the surface for a soft hiss of air.

Roll

Then submerge the tip and spin the milk into a smooth whirlpool until it folds in on itself.

Heat

Stop at 60 to 65 degrees: the jug is almost too hot to hold. Never boil, it scorches and kills the sweetness.

Fill the jug just below the spout, and swirl it before every pour so it stays glossy.

Every milk, and how to foam it

Two things decide it. Protein builds the walls of the bubbles, so it sets structure and stability. Fat makes the foam creamy and rounds out the taste. More protein means a steadier foam; more fat means more creaminess. Barista editions are tuned for exactly this.

Whole cow's milk

The benchmark, and the most forgiving. Its high protein builds a fine, stable foam, and the fat makes it creamy and lightly sweet. Handles beginner mistakes best of all.

Protein3.4 g / 100 ml
Fat3.5 g
FoamExcellent, stable

Tip Cold and fresh from the fridge. The easiest choice for latte art.

Oat, barista edition

The best plant option, closest to milk. Naturally lower in protein, but barista editions add a little oil and stabilisers, so they foam almost like milk. Naturally sweet and easy against espresso.

Protein~1.0 g / 100 ml
Fat~1.5 to 3 g
FoamExcellent (barista only)

Tip Always the barista version. Steam a touch cooler. Plain oat barely foams.

Soy

The highest-protein plant milk, so it foams well and holds its shape. The catch: the protein can curdle against hot, acidic espresso.

Protein~3.3 g / 100 ml
Fat~1.8 g
FoamGood, can curdle

Tip Steam cooler (max 60 degrees) and pour promptly, then it will not split.

Almond and the rest

Nutty and light, but the trickiest to foam: little protein means thin, airy foam that collapses fast and separates from the coffee.

Protein~0.5 g / 100 ml
Fat~1.1 g
FoamThin, unstable

Tip Barista edition only, low heat, pour straight away.

Best cow's milk: whole, fresh and cold. Its protein and fat build the steadiest foam; low-fat froths bigger but drier and falls apart faster.

With any plant milk: use a barista edition, steam cooler (around 55 to 60 degrees), and wipe and purge the wand after, they scorch fast.

The patterns

Four to learn, in order.

Each one builds on the last. Master the heart before you chase the swan.

Heart

Beginner

The starting point for everything. You pour a round white pool into the centre, then slice through it to pull the tip.

  1. Pour the textured milk in from a height into the tilted cup until it is about half full and still mostly brown.
  2. Lower the spout almost onto the surface and pour steadily into one spot. A white circle blooms and rises.
  3. When the circle nearly fills the cup, lift and cut a thin stream straight through its middle to the near edge. That pulls the point.

Rosetta

Intermediate

The classic leaf. A steady side-to-side wobble of the jug lays down rows of leaves, then one line ties them together.

  1. Build the brown base as before, then bring the spout down close near the far side of the cup.
  2. Start a small, even wobble left and right and very slowly drift the jug back toward you. Each wobble drops a leaf, and they fan out.
  3. At the near edge, stop wobbling, lift, and draw one thin line straight back through the centre. That is the stem.

Tulip

Intermediate

Stacked hearts. You pour several separate pushes of white that shove into each other, then finish with a heart on top.

  1. On the brown base, drop close and pour a small round blob, then stop the pour completely.
  2. Pour a second blob just behind the first; it pushes the first one forward into a bump. Repeat two or three times.
  3. For the last one, pour a slightly bigger heart and cut a line straight through all the layers to a single point.

Swan

Advanced

The showpiece, and really a rosetta plus a heart. One side becomes the feathered wing, a thin line the neck, a small heart the head.

  1. Pour a rosetta into one half of the cup. That is the wing and the tail of the swan.
  2. From the top of the wing, lift slightly and drag a long, thin, curved line out and around. That draws the neck.
  3. At the end of the neck, drop close, pour a small round blob and cut through it. That is the head and beak.

Troubleshooting

When it goes wrong.

The white sinks, no pattern

You poured too high or too early. Get the spout closer to the surface before the white should show.

Big bubbles on top

Too much air, or air added too late. Stretch only in the first seconds, then submerge the tip. Tap the jug and swirl to pop bubbles.

Foam too stiff to pour

Over-stretched. You want wet paint, not meringue. Less air, more spinning.

No contrast, all pale

The base was not blended in. Start higher and thinner to mix with the crema before you drop close.
