Latte art

Pour like the counter.

A free, no-nonsense guide to milk, microfoam, and the four patterns worth learning. From people who pour every day.

Fundamentals

It is won or lost in the milk.

Latte art is not wrist magic. It is texture. Get the milk to wet paint, glossy and fine, and the pattern almost pours itself. Everything below matters more than the pour.

01

The milk

Cold, fresh whole milk is easiest; its proteins build the most stable foam. Barista oat is the best non-dairy option and steams close to milk. Start with a cold jug, filled just below the spout.

02

Microfoam

Stretch first: tip just below the surface for a short hiss of air, two to three seconds. Then submerge the tip to spin the milk into a smooth whirlpool and heat to about 60 to 65 degrees. No big bubbles, just a glossy sheen.

03

The pour

Swirl the milk before pouring so it stays glossy. Start high and thin to blend into the crema, then drop the spout close to the surface to let the white float. Height hides the pattern, closeness reveals it.

The patterns

Four to learn, in order.

Each one builds on the last. Master the heart before you chase the swan.

Heart

Beginner

The foundation of every pattern. Pour a round white pool, then cut straight through it to pull the point.

  1. Fill the cup to about half with a high, thin stream.
  2. Drop close and pour on one spot until a white circle grows and rises.
  3. Lift and draw a thin stream straight through the middle to the far edge.

Rosetta

Intermediate

The leaf. A gentle side-to-side wiggle lays down stacked leaves, then one line ties them together.

  1. Base as before, then drop close near the far rim and start a small, steady wiggle.
  2. Wiggle in place while slowly drawing the jug back toward you; the leaves fan out.
  3. Stop wiggling, lift, and draw one line straight through to the front for the stem.

Tulip

Intermediate

Stacked pushes. Several pulses of white pushed into each other, then capped with a heart.

  1. Pour a small blob close to the surface, then stop.
  2. Pour a second blob just behind it; the first gets pushed forward. Repeat two or three times.
  3. Finish with a heart on top and cut through all the layers to a single point.

Swan

Advanced

The showpiece. A rosetta for the wing, a curved pour for the neck, a small heart for the head.

  1. Pour a rosetta to one side of the cup as the wing and tail.
  2. From the wing, draw a long thin curved line out and around for the neck.
  3. At the end of the neck, pour and cut a small heart for the head.

Troubleshooting

When it goes wrong.

The white sinks, no pattern

You poured too high or too early. Get the spout closer to the surface before the white should show.

Big bubbles on top

Too much air, or air added too late. Stretch only in the first seconds, then submerge the tip. Tap the jug and swirl to pop bubbles.

Foam too stiff to pour

Over-stretched. You want wet paint, not meringue. Less air, more spinning.

No contrast, all pale

The base was not blended in. Start higher and thinner to mix with the crema before you drop close.
